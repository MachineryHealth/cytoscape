Introduction

Cells face a steady input of signals. All cellular decisions, like survival or apoptosis (cellular suicide), proliferation or secretion of certain compounds are goverened by this complex pattern of inputs (or lack thereof). Obviously, the cell needs a way to map the set of inputs to the set of possible responses, weighting and integrating the different inputs in their context. It has to be possible for the cell to adapt to the multitude of exterior environments. Therefore the signaling network has to be robust, that means it may not fall apart because if minute changes in some parameter. To achieve this, differing levels of exterior have to be translated into one cellular state, which may change to another one on a certain treshold in the signal. This many-to-one mapping (see Somogyi ) is an essential ingredient in life.

This integration is the product of mechanisms on at least three levels:

  1. the set of receptors, which act as a first signal filter
  2. the transduction pathways hooked up to them and their interaction, which may act as a complex switching network (but also may turn out to do more or less linear passing on of the signal). In the context of this document, a signal transduction pathway is the series of molecular interactions triggered by an extracellular signaling molecule and leading to the activation of one or more transcription factors.
  3. finally the regulatory region on the DNA, which acts as a regulatory program for transcription factors, and the current set of expressed transcription factors.

While the first and last point mostly depend on expression patterns of the components, the transduction networks also strongly depend of the interactions which connect the signaling molecules. Connectionism is the notion that complex networks of simple elements show emergent behavior, which is a property of the network, not of those simple elements per se. This complex behaviour stems mainly from feedback lopps, where elements downstream in a signaling pathway influence elements upstream of themselves.

A global view of signal transduction would include the gene-regulatory networks in the nucleus and the intercellular signaling molecules also, yielding an organisma wide signaling network, with interactions on vastly different timescales. These different timescales can be a means for the organism of decoupling slow signaling from the state of fast-changing parts of the networks, since the slow processes will only see an average of the fast ones [].

Properties and mechanisms

Which signaling mechanisms are essential for reproducing behaviour of a intracellular signaling system? Several likely candidates include feedback loops, signal amplification and treshholds, timing delays and self- modification of the network wiring.

Feedback circuits

Each element of a feedback circuit (or loop) has an direct or indirect effect on itself. The circuit is said to have positive feedback, when this effect is positive, otherwise the circuit is said to have negative feedback. The sign is easily determined by checking the parity of negative interactions. If this is zero or even, the circuit is positive, otherwise it is negative. Positive circuits can yield multistationary behaviour, negative ones can generate damped or sustained oszillatory behaviour and homeostasis.[Mendoza99][Thieffry and Romero 99]. The effect of a circuit does not only depend on the existence of the interactions, but also their relative strengths: a functional circuit is one with such parameters, that the effect will be multistationarity or homeostasis. [Thieffry and Romero 99] suggest that the larger the circuit, the bigger the set of constraints it has to fullfill, and therefore most circuits should be very short, one or two elements only in most cases. While this may hold for gene- regulatioory circuits, clearly it is not true for some metabolic and regulatory [Bhalla and Lyengar 99] circuits.

  • Positive feedback loops: Switches and Memory effects

    Positive feedback is a mechanism to change the mode of activation of a component from proportional to the signal strength to an "off" state below and an "on" state above a certain signal level. In this model a product downstream of the activated component also activates the component, leading to it's own production, activating the component even more etc. If the signal grows beyond a level where this positive feedback can kick in, then the activation will suddenly take off, independent of the input signal level. Another way to change linear growing response to sigmoid growing response (which looks very much like on/off, when it happens fast enough) is allosteric influences, like you got with haemoglobins O2 binding: At a low concentration, the ligand binds poorly. But Bound subtrate changes the conformation of the - often symmetric - carrier as to bind better. This leads to much faster binding at higher concentration. At even higher concentrations, when all sites are saturated with ligand, the curve ends in a plateau, giving an overall sigmoid shape with an "off" state at low concentrations, then a fast change as concentration changes to an "on" state at higher ones.

    During development transient signals can effect a permanent change in the cells state. A mechanism to allow these memory effects is again positive feedback: once the treshhold is surpassed, the component will stay activated, by reactivating itself. The only way to get back to the previous state, then, is another signal that breaks the positive feedback loop. Another way to change the cell is by activating transcription and thus introducing new players into the network, which change it's behaviour.

  • Negative feedback: Robustness of signal level

    A mechanism to keep the level of any compound in a certain range is negative feedback loops. If the concentration of a compound exeeds the upper limit, the comound itself stops the process leading to its production, if it falls below the lower limit, another pathway activating it's production is switched on (cf. glucose/glycogen levels). Negative does not neccesarily mean switching something off, but ratehr acting in the opposite direction of the observed change.

Amplification

Cascades are a mechanism to amplify signals. If on each step in an enzymatic cascade ten enzymes of the next level are activated by phosphorylation, then you will have multiplied the signal a thousandfold after three steps. This amplification is paid for by ATP burned up in the phosphorylations. Amplification is not limited to this mechanism. Binding to a channel which releases large amounts of Calcium from the ER is another example of amplification, this time paid for by the energy used to build up the Calcium cooncentration gradient over the ER membrane.

Time delays

Turnover rates are a mechanism to control the speed of the response. Biological signals act on a network whose components are subject to constant degradation and builtup. To keep a steady concentration of a molecule, the production rate has to match the degradation rate. The amount of molecules exchanged per unit of time is called the turnover rate. If you switch off the signal, it's effect is flushed by exchanging modified molecules through new unmodified ones. A high turnover will lead to fast signal shutdown. But it also can lead to fast response to the signal: if the signal leads for example to doubling the production rate of a molecule, then that molecules level will rise much faster in system with the high turnover. Systems which have to respond fast thus often show a high turnover rate. The extra energy burned in those "futile" cycles is the price to pay for the ability of fast response.

Self-modification

The mechanism by which the cellular signling machinery can re-wire itself is transcription. If a signal leads to transcription of a protein not present before, then after the signal you have another signalling network. This differnce my be only superfical, as you could see the proteins of all genes as present in any cell, albeit in an inactive state, which changes through transcription to another (presumably active) one.

Static modeling

One has to differentiate between the static model of the network itself and the dynamic model of what goes on in this network. The static model contains different states of signaling compounds and their wiring. By wiring I mean the connections between these compounds, including additional information like if a connection is activating or inhibiting, or it's rate constant.

This model is essentialy a big graph, where the nodes are states of signaling compounds and their interactions to each other. Depending on the detail level of modeling, on can have represent the network as a clique grape with only one type of node (the signaling compounds), where the interactions are implicitly contained in the edges, as a bipartite graph, where the interactions also form nodes or even as a tripartite graph, where the conections between interactions and compounds form nodes. It may be convenient to view the bipartite graph as a hypergraph, where reactions are the hyperedges.

The static model can stored in a database.

If we want to model a signaling reaction in the cytosol, we usually have two or three signaling inputs, either two molecules that interact directly, or aided by an enzyme. (If one were to model the enzymatic reaction in detail, this would in most cases reduce to two sequential reactions between two molecules for each case). Combinatoric richness in this case comes from several reactions competing for the same molecules.

If we want to simulate an promotor, it has to be possible to express boolean relations between binding factors, since some can activate, others inhibit, these may compete and the overall outcome of the decision to transcribe may well depend on the relative numbers of activators and inhibitors bound. In the graph we have a group of active promotor states, from which the transcription reaction is wired, and a group of inactive ones, from which it is not. By connecting these states with all possible sequences of binding reactions all states will be enumerated and connected, and there is no need to built any kind of boolean information is stored in the reactions and the state distribution. With a big number of states, everything can be modeled in a plain graph, but this is impractical for the example just discussed. By using multiple inputs for each edge, we can build a hypergraph that is much easier to handle.

The decision which factors will bind in a real-life situation will depend on the concentrations of factors, and on the binding constants. An inhibitor with binding constant for binding will quickly claim the available promotor binding site, making it unavailable for an activating transcription factor with a low constant.

Thus actual competition or inhibition decisions based on the mechanistic model can only be seen during actual simulation of the mechanism. In contrast, when building networks of activation/inhibition relations (semantic model), it may be possible to predict certain properties from the wiring alone.

One can, however also try to built this boolean information into the reaction instead into the state distribution. In this case, and AND will be all the reactants and enzymes needed, a NOT will be the inhibitors. An OR would have to be modeled by a group as a reactant. The model is limited, since it does not allow for arbitrary constructs but only for constructs matching the syntax

reaction   ::= activators | activators "AND NOT" inhibitors
activators ::= alternative | alternative "AND" activators
inhibitors ::= alternative | alternative "AND" inhibitors
alternative::= molecule | molecule "OR" alternative
Also, one needs contextual information for the molecules and reactions, like stoichometric indices, which wouold correspond to atrributes for the relations-arrows in a petrinet.

Ways to model dynamic regulatory networks

There are two generally differing ways to model these networks: one, first build the model with a priori parameters from expert knowledge. If all the parameters and connections were correct, and thus the model is correct, and the model is expressive enough, then the model should predict observed data. Thus data is used only for hypothesis testing about the model. If the model does not predict data correctly, it then has to be improved by hand - and it may be not obvious where to change it, if it shows complex behaviour. Two, automatically infer the model (or at least its parameters) from the data using machine learning techniques. After you train a model with an unbiased training set, it then should be able to predict regulation of a test set. The disadvantages are that you may need a lot of date, and that today's expression data measures RNA levels. It has been shown that for some cells the correlation between expressed RNA and Proteins is only about 50%[]. Also, important proteins which are only needed in low concentration (like transcription factors) may be entirely missed.

A good compromise seems to be: build the model by hand, then train it with data to infer or improve parameters. Thus the model wiring works as a contraint on the machine learning. If the model cannot be fitted to the data, there may be some mistake in the models wiring, which then has to be invetigated experimentally.

Data classes usable for modeling

In order of decreasing detail and modeling power:
  • kinetic data enzymatic speed constants, reaction equilibrium constants, concentrations of molecules (these last of course would have to represent some steady state in a given cell and tissue type of a given developmental stage, probably they can only be given for the adult stage, or would have to be given as a time series.) Kinetics also need stoichometric coefficients
  • semantic data what is the effect and context of a (boolean) interaction? is it inhibition, activation, is it direct, or indirect?
  • connectivity data which molecule interacts with which other molecule

Further properties of on the model

  • Partitioning: How far can subnets be seen as modules, and the net be constructed from subnets?
  • Time-delay: how can we include timing delays. Time delays (especial signal accumulation delays) are central to the correct function of the circuit [McAdams & Shapiro 95]. Do we have a synchronized clock or independend concurrent flows of events? How can we handle the vastly different timescales of various processes? Do we need to?

Timing

Time delay mechanisms, especially transcription delay and signal accumulation delays, are central to the correct functioning of the circuits. (Sharipo)

Asynchronity

All cellular communication proceeds locally and asynchronous time, as there is no means for global synchronisation.

Modularization and decoupling

Once you start building a network like this, people come and say "Gee, what a waste of time. In the end you'll find everything is connected to everything else. It is. But it still works. One of the most interesting things to find out is how the decoupling of signals takes part, even though the pathways are interconnected into networks.

One mechanism might be spatial compartimentation. It's like using modules in programming (and actually object-oriented programming incorporates ideas seen in biological systems to handel complexity (cf. Booch, Hartwell). if you have subsystems with strong interactions or coupling within, but weaker interactions or coupling between subsystems, the overall system is much more robust, as changes in one subsystem do not influence the others strongly.

Also, eukaryotic cells have evolved scaffolding proteins that can simultaneously bind multiple components of a signaling pathway and thereby impose a degree of specificity and order on he highly connected network of signaling molecules.

Compartimentation does not stop at the subcellular compartment level: often molecules in a cascade are grouped together by scaffold or adapter molecules. One example is the yeast pheromone response with Ste5/ste11/Ste7/Fus3-Kss1, which is homolog to the human MAP Kinase cascade. Clustering insulates these molecules and may diminuish 'cross- talk' to other pathways. It also makes possible a new degree of signal amplification and speed which could not be achieved with molecules freely distributed in the cytosol and relying on diffusion to collide and interact every now and then. (The notion of the cytosol as a thin solution akin to an aquatic one is quite wrong anyways, it's much more like a gel, filled with cytoskeletal structures which also act as rails for transport processes.)

Another may be time-scale decoupling (Rodjestvenski), times of processes in the cell vary significantly from <> in enzymatic catalysis or even femtoseconds in photosyntetic excitation to dozens of minutes for protein expression and even days or weeks for hormone responses. Slower processes are insensitive to the values of fast-changing processes, seeing only their averaged value.

Previous work: network models and tools

As far as modeling is concerned, what has already been tried? Which research groups exist, and what is their approach? What kinds of models for networks exist? What kind of data is needed to support the various models?

Network models of all kinds have been used on such research topics as electrical circuitry, gene-regeulatory networks or metabolic networks. Since we are interested ways to model networks, we present an overview based on the kind of model used, not on the kind of application domain. The classification of methods follows [Hofest臈t and Thelen 98].

Analytical models: quantitative simulation
These are the most detailed models, nedd the most parameters and have to date been used most often. Because of their hunger for information and compuztaional complexity, usually only small systems can be modeled.
微分方程式 differential equations
Method The network is modeled as a collection of (usually nonlinear) differnetial equations, where reaction rates and compound concentratuions are the variables. These are solved then numerically for each time step. Stoichometric coefficients, starting concentrations and rate constants for all interactions are needed.
Advantages Of course, quantitative modeling would give the highest predictive precision. The concept of sensitivity of a pathway (i.e. the change in level of activated target divided by the change in the level of signal) allows the quantification of signal transfer.
LimitationsUnfortunately, the conditions under which the parameters for the model have been measured differ widely. Unless the system is robust vs. those differences, it'll not be possible to reconstruct the actual workings of the network. Also for large networks the computational cost may get very high.
Example Applications [Kholodenko97]

There is a far more mature theory and more databases for metabolic networks and their analysis, which has been largely modeled with differntial equations.
How far can such tools be used for modeling regulatory networks?
In metabolic networks the model is that of a small molecule that is transformed by enzymatically catalyzed reactions. Thus there is always a flow of mass through the network. Nothing is said about the regulation of the enzymes - if they are present, they are assumed to work. In regulatory networks, information is flowing, and often does so without mass being passed on. Consider the catalysis in regulatory cascades: here you have futile cycles between the active and inactive state of the enzymes from a mass- conservation point of view, but what really counts is the passing of information that manifests in this ste-change. These processes will not yield usable results with present day tools for metabolic network analysis.
離散モデル Discrete models: 定性的 シミュレーション qualitative simulation
These models do not need exact concentrations. They represent a middle ground between the more exhaustive differential equation systems and the very simolified abstrart models.
Petrinets
Advantages Petri Nets have a mature theoretical background, and numerous tools for analysis and running simulations exist. There are a lot of variants, including colored and timed Petrinets. There are properties which can be checked for petrinets, like liveness or reachability.
Limitations The main limitation is that of most of the discrete models, transitions switch, or they don't, there are no probabilities. Also given the big number of tools it is disappointing they do not use a common data exchange format.
Example Applications [Hofest臈t and Thelen 98] used Petri Nets to simulate metabolic networks.
Stochastic Activity Nets (SANs)
Advantages These are a variant of Petrinets, which put more muscle in the conditions upon which transactions fire, thereby making it easier to model behavior witthout introducing many additional places. Switching here is based on probabilities, overcoming one limitation most other discrete models have.
Limitations There are still only discrete sets of tokens moved in the network (although one could try to assimilate floating-point behaviour by large numbers of tokens, so that one token in effect equals the smalles possible fraction of a unit to be represented).
Example Applications [Mounts and Liebman 97] used a SAN model blood coagulation.
Neural Networks/Parallel Distributed Process-networks (PDP)
Method Layered network of neural units with input layer, hidden inner layers, output layer plus training program for weights (i.e. neural network approach). Compounds = neural units.
Needed values Connectivity, input/output functions (reactions) for compounds, connection weights.
Predictive power
Example Applications [Bray90]
Rule-based systems (Prolog, Lisp, or Expert System shells)
MethodThe network is modeled as a collection of IF..THEN .. rules. A rule fires, when the boolean exression in its preconditions (IF part) holds, setting the values in its postconditions (THEN part) to TRUE. There are effecient algorithms (like RETE) to re-evaluate if a rule fires when some arbitrary rukle of the network is changed. For Parameters, connectivity is enough for the base level. Depending on the state of the cell conditions have to be initialized to TRUE.. Rules are our Reactions and fire when all compounds and enzymes are present and the inhibitors are not.
Advantages Like the boolean networks needs only little data to begin with, and has prebuild tools to do the computation. (Rule- based models seem to be a transform of boolean nets? The principal difference seems to be that the boolean networks handle bitstrings, while this handles symbolic names, maybe more appropriate to our task.)
Limitations See Boolean Networks.
Example Applications [Brutlag et al. 91] used it to model enzyme behaviour (for DNA polymerase).
Abstract models: cellular automata and logical models
Cellular automata use the content of cells in a matrix to calculate the content of neighboring cells. If the influencing cells are definde by spacial proximity (like in the "Game of Life" automaton), you have a normal automaton, otherwise you have a boolean network. Varieties of cellular automata have been used to study complex systems: von Neumann and Ulam, Conway, Wolfram, Langton.
(Random) Boolean Networks
Method This is the simplest model, but still is able to express positive and negative feedback loops. Parameters you need are a set of rules and a set of tokens that defines which cells are on in each iteration of the network. For details see here.
Advantages Computationally cheap and has been extensively used before.
Limitations Since the model is boolean only, no quantities can be expressed. Thus amplification and thresholds cannot be modeled.
Example Applications

Gene-regulatory Networks:[Thieffry & Romero 99]

VLSI Design: This is about modeling of electric signal networks. A lot of the ideas and methods could be used in our context, too. Naturally there are models of all kinds used in that context, but the gate-level logic models seem to be most interesting. [McAdams & Shapiro 95] used this approach by modeling gene-regulatory networks, mixing in nonlinear differential equations for selected critical control elements.

Open Questions

Modeling Questions

The problem with the state machine approach is that state is contextually dependend on the location of the molecule. In the nucleus a transcription factor can activate transcription, but in the cytosol it obviously can not. Since there are so many different possible contexts, this can theoretically explode the number of states, just think also about developmental stages, species, different cell types. Also if one molecule can be phosphorylated at a lot of sites, like PDGFR, the number of possible combinations is exploding. The limiting factor for the database is of course what was experimentally shown. But with experimental science shifting to bulk-data aquisition, like it is done with chip technologies, this limit may well fall.

The number of molecules and locales involved just makes it impossible to really model the whole cell as a big state machine.

Similarly the possible combinations of factors binding to a promotor and the boolean rules that govern if the promoter will be activated or not are not well modelled in a state transition net. For a promotor with n different factors able to bind to the m binding sites, one could have m to the power of n + 1 (for none binding) different states. And only some of them would activate transcription. This could be expressed much more succinct with a boolean rule for the sites. The other way to avoid the problem is to group the factors by function, effectively reducing their numbers.

General Questions

How important are signal transduction pathways for the specificity of the cells response to a signal? Is all of the different behaviour between cells because of the differently expressed receptors and factors? If, so is it still useful to model signaling networks?

There are only a relatively small number of known intracellular pathways for signal transduction, and these also do cross-talk. The current notion[Pawson and Saxton 99] is that the function of these networks is to integrate incoming signals. Several positive signals add up to a higher quantitative effect in immediate early gene transcription, and a coherent response to conflicting signals is made possible. Since all kinds of receptors link to those few pathways, the activation of distinct signaling pathways at the membrane may converge on a related set of promotors. The question here is how biological specificity can be generated through rather general intracellular signals. The current notion is that cells with different histories, and therefore expressing different repertoires of transcription factors may form distinct complexes of newly activated transcription factors, which target different promotors. The same signal may therefore induce different transcriptional readouts in different cells.

Oncogenes often code for key proteins in signal transduction pathways, which then constitutively switch on transcription. Having information about alternate pathways and the interaction between pathways will help to understand abnormal behaviour and to target critical elements in pathways for medical treatment. Therfore, modelimng the networks should be interesting even if most of the specificity of responses roots in a cells history and in the expression pattern of the entries and exits from the signal transduction maze.

The basic idea to model cellular signaling in the computer is to have the cell as a big state machine, with each signalling molecule another, smaller state machine, which can change frome one state to another upon recieving signals, and which transmits signals to other molecules (and sometimes itself), depending of the state it is in. So you end up with a big graph (or Petri-Net) of molecule-states and interaction-reactions. In the simplest case only two states (on/off) for each molecule would exist, so you could describe it with a single node reciving and sending activation/inhibition signals (the semantic view of the network).
